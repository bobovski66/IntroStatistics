Math 146 — Week 10 Prequiz Practice

Linear regression, correlation, \(r^2\), residual analysis, and regression from raw data. Expand each solution to check your work.

Reference Formulas

Slope and intercept: \[b = r\cdot\frac{s_y}{s_x} \qquad a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x} \qquad \hat{y} = a + bx\]

Residual: \(e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\)  ·  \(r^2\) = proportion of variability in \(y\) explained by the linear relationship with \(x\).

The regression line always passes through \((\bar{x},\,\bar{y})\).

Problem 1 — Computing the Regression Line

A physical therapist records weeks of therapy (\(x\)) and improvement score (\(y\)) for 10 patients: \(\bar{x}=6\), \(s_x=2.5\), \(\bar{y}=40\), \(s_y=12\), \(r=0.85\).

  1. Compute the least-squares regression line \(\hat{y} = a + bx\).
  2. Predict the improvement score for 8 weeks of therapy.
  3. Compute \(r^2\) and interpret it in context.
  4. A residual plot shows a curved pattern. What does this suggest?
Show Solution
  1. \[b = 0.85 \times \frac{12}{2.5} = 0.85 \times 4.8 = 4.08\] \[a = 40 - 4.08(6) = 40 - 24.48 = 15.52\]
    Answer: \(\hat{y} = 15.52 + 4.08x\). For each additional week of therapy, improvement score is predicted to increase by 4.08 points on average.
  2. \[\hat{y}(8) = 15.52 + 4.08(8) = 15.52 + 32.64 = 48.16\]
    Answer: \(\hat{y} = 48.16\) points. Since 8 weeks is within the data range, this prediction is reasonable.
  3. \[r^2 = (0.85)^2 = 0.7225\]
    Answer: \(r^2 = 0.7225\). About 72.25% of the variability in improvement score is explained by the linear relationship with weeks of therapy. The remaining 27.75% reflects other factors — age, injury severity, adherence, etc.
  4. Answer: A curved residual plot means the linearity condition is violated — the true relationship is not linear. A non-linear model (e.g., quadratic or logarithmic) would likely fit better.

Problem 2 — Interpreting Regression in Context

50 cities: average summer temperature (\(x\), °F) and ice cream sales per capita (\(y\), dollars). Line: \(\hat{y} = -120 + 4.5x\), \(r^2 = 0.61\). Warmest city in data: \(x = 95\)°F.

  1. Interpret the slope in context.
  2. Interpret \(r^2 = 0.61\) in context.
  3. Is predicting at \(x = 110\)°F appropriate? Why or why not?
  4. A journalist concludes: "Eating ice cream raises body temperature." What is wrong?
Show Solution
  1. Answer: For each additional degree Fahrenheit in average summer temperature, annual ice cream sales per capita are predicted to increase by $4.50.
  2. Answer: 61% of the variability in annual ice cream sales across cities is explained by the linear relationship with average summer temperature. The remaining 39% is due to other factors — income, population density, local culture, etc.
  3. Answer: No — this is extrapolation. The data only go up to \(x = 95\)°F. Predicting at \(x = 110\)°F uses the line beyond the range where it was validated, and the linear pattern may not hold there.
  4. Answer: This confuses correlation with causation. Both ice cream sales and body temperature rise in summer because of hot weather — a confounding variable. The correlation does not mean one causes the other.

Problem 5 — The Closing Thread (Essay)

Write a paragraph (4–6 sentences) tracing the central question — "what does one variable tell you about another?" — from Chapter 1 through Chapter 10. Name at least five tools.

Show Model Answer
Model Answer: This course begins with a single variable: the mean locates its center and the standard deviation measures its spread, together quantifying how much a single observation tells us about a typical value. Moving to two variables, the scatterplot reveals the shape of a relationship visually, and the correlation coefficient \(r\) summarizes its direction and linear strength numerically. The least-squares regression line makes the relationship actionable — it is the best linear predictor of \(y\) from \(x\), and its slope tells us how much \(y\) changes per unit increase in \(x\). The coefficient of determination \(r^2\) sharpens this picture by giving the fraction of variability in the response explained by the predictor, converting a correlation into a concrete statement about predictive power. For categorical variables, the chi-squared test asks whether two variables are associated at all, playing the same structural role as correlation does for quantitative variables — both tools answer, at their respective levels of measurement, the course's central question: how much does one thing tell us about another?

What to Watch For

slope formulaintercept formula \(r^2\) interpretationextrapolation residual plotcorrelation ≠ causation raw data regression
  • The regression line always passes through \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\). Verify: plug in \(\bar{x}\) and confirm you get \(\bar{y}\).
  • \(r^2\) is always between 0 and 1 regardless of the sign of \(r\). Squaring removes the sign.
  • Interpret the slope with units: "for each one-unit increase in [x], \(\hat{y}\) changes by [slope] [units of y]."
  • Extrapolation means predicting outside the range of the data — the linear pattern is not validated there.
  • A curved residual plot signals the linearity condition is violated — the model is inappropriate, not just imprecise.
  • When computing regression from raw data with a calculator, verify your result makes directional sense by checking whether the sign of the slope agrees with the direction of association in the data.
