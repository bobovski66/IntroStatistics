Week 2 Summary

Probability Distributions as Models

This week we move from “what we saw in one sample” to “a model for the whole population.” Continuous curves and discrete tables are both ways of describing probability distributions.

1. From Histograms to Probability Distributions

In Week 1, we used histograms and summary numbers (like $\bar x$ and $s$) to describe data from one sample.

In Week 2, we treat a probability distribution as a model for the whole population:

  • It tells us which values are more or less likely.
  • It lets us talk about long-run probabilities.
  • It has its own population mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$.

We will use both:

  • Continuous distributions: smooth curves where probability is area under the curve.
  • Discrete distributions: probability tables and bar graphs where each value gets its own probability.
Running examples for this week:
  • Continuous: $X =$ resting heart rate (beats per minute) for a randomly chosen student.
  • Discrete: $Y =$ number of tutoring center visits in a week for a randomly chosen student.

The continuous example uses a smooth curve. The discrete example uses a probability table.

2. Continuous Distributions: Density Curves and Area

Density curves as population models

A continuous probability distribution is described by a smooth curve lying above the number line. We think of it as a population model:

  • Horizontal axis: possible values of $X$ (e.g., heart rates).
  • Vertical axis: density (how crowded the population is near each value).
  • Total area under the curve is $1$ (100% of the probability).
A bell-shaped density curve for resting heart rate. Higher parts of the curve correspond to more common values; the total area under the curve is 1.

Area under the curve = probability

If $X$ is resting heart rate, then $P(60 \le X \le 80)$ is the area under the curve between 60 and 80 beats per minute:

The shaded region under the curve represents $P(60 \le X \le 80)$: the probability that a randomly chosen student’s heart rate lies in that interval.

We interpret $P(60 \le X \le 80)$ as the proportion of all students whose resting heart rates fall between 60 and 80 bpm.

3. Mean and Standard Deviation in Continuous Models

Mean as a balance point

For a continuous distribution, the population mean $\mu$ is like a balance point:

  • Imagine the density curve is a thin metal sheet.
  • $\mu$ is the point where it would balance on a seesaw.

In calculus language (not required for the course), the mean is \[ \mu = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x)\,dx, \] where $f(x)$ is the height of the density curve.

Standard deviation as width

The population standard deviation $\sigma$ controls the spread of the curve:

  • small $\sigma$ $\Rightarrow$ narrow, tall curve (values tightly clustered),
  • large $\sigma$ $\Rightarrow$ wide, flat curve (values more spread out).
Both curves are centered at the same mean. The darker curve is more tightly concentrated around the mean (smaller $\sigma$); the lighter curve is more spread out (larger $\sigma$).
4. Discrete Distributions: Probability Tables and Means

Discrete random variables

A discrete random variable takes isolated values, often counts: 0,1,2,3,$\dots$.

Our example: $Y =$ number of tutoring center visits in a week by a randomly chosen student.

A discrete probability table

Suppose the population behaves like this:

$y$ (visits) 012345
$P(Y=y)$ 0.400.300.150.080.050.02

Check the total probability: \[ 0.40 + 0.30 + 0.15 + 0.08 + 0.05 + 0.02 = 1.00. \]

Discrete probability distribution for weekly tutoring visits: the height of each bar is $P(Y=y)$.

Computing probabilities and the mean

From the table:

  • $P(Y = 0) = 0.40$ (40% of students do not visit at all).
  • $P(Y \ge 2) = P(Y=2)+P(Y=3)+P(Y=4)+P(Y=5) = 0.15+0.08+0.05+0.02 = 0.30$.

The population mean of this discrete distribution is a weighted average: \[ \mu_Y = \sum_y y \cdot P(Y=y). \]

For this table: \[ \begin{aligned} \mu_Y &= 0\cdot 0.40 + 1\cdot 0.30 + 2\cdot 0.15 + 3\cdot 0.08 + 4\cdot 0.05 + 5\cdot 0.02 \\ &= 0 + 0.30 + 0.30 + 0.24 + 0.20 + 0.10 = 1.14. \end{aligned} \]

Interpretation: in the long run, the average number of weekly visits per student is 1.14.

5. Empirical Distributions and Sampling Variability

From population model to sample data

The probability table for $Y$ is a population model. In real life, we see only samples.

Suppose we sample $n = 10$ students and record their weekly tutoring visits: \[ 0,\ 0,\ 1,\ 1,\ 1,\ 2,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 5. \]

Empirical (sample) distribution

Turn the sample into a frequency and relative-frequency table:

$y$ (visits) 012345
Frequency 232111
Relative freq. 0.200.300.200.100.100.10

Compare to the population probabilities:

012345
Population $P(Y=y)$ 0.400.300.150.080.050.02
Sample rel. freq. 0.200.300.200.100.100.10

They are not identical, but they are similar: some sample bars are higher, some lower. This is normal.

Sample mean vs population mean

Sample mean from the 10 students: \[ \bar y = \frac{0+0+1+1+1+2+2+3+4+5}{10} = \frac{19}{10} = 1.9. \]

Compare:

  • Population mean: $\mu_Y = 1.14$ (from the probability table).
  • Sample mean: $\bar y = 1.9$ (from this particular sample).

They differ because a sample is only a subset of the population. If we took a different random sample of 10 students, we would get a different $\bar y$. This natural wobbling of sample statistics is called sampling variability.

6. Big Picture for Week 2

By the end of Week 2, you should be comfortable with:

  • Reading a density curve as a population model where area under the curve = probability.
  • Interpreting the population mean $\mu$ as a center and the population standard deviation $\sigma$ as the width (spread) of the distribution.
  • Reading a discrete probability table and computing probabilities like $P(Y \ge 2)$.
  • Computing the mean of a discrete distribution as a weighted average $\mu = \sum y \cdot P(Y=y)$.
  • Understanding that samples have empirical distributions and sample statistics ($\bar x$, $s$), which vary from sample to sample and generally differ from the population model.

In upcoming weeks, we will apply these ideas to specific named distributions like the uniform, normal, geometric, and binomial.
