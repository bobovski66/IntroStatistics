Probability Distributions as Models

Try each problem before opening the solution. Focus on what the distribution is saying about the population, and how the sample numbers relate to that model.

Problem 1: Waiting Time for the Campus Shuttle

Suppose the time $T$ (in minutes) a student waits for a particular campus shuttle is modeled by a continuous probability distribution with a smooth curve on the interval from 0 to 20 minutes.

The curve is shaped so that:

  • Very short waits (0–2 minutes) are rare.
  • Moderate waits (5–10 minutes) are most common.
  • Very long waits (15–20 minutes) are rare again.

Think of this as a “bump” centered between 5 and 10 minutes, with total area 1 under the curve.

  1. In words, what does $P(5 \le T \le 10)$ represent in this model?
  2. If the area under the curve from 5 to 10 minutes is 0.6, what is $P(5 \le T \le 10)$?
  3. If the area from 0 to 5 minutes is 0.2, what is $P(10 < T \le 20)$? Explain how you got your answer.
Show solution

(a) Interpreting $P(5 \le T \le 10)$

$P(5 \le T \le 10)$ is the probability that a randomly chosen student’s waiting time is between 5 and 10 minutes. In long-run language, it is the proportion of all shuttle waits that fall between 5 and 10 minutes.

(b) Area as probability

If the area under the curve from 5 to 10 is 0.6, then \[ P(5 \le T \le 10) = 0.6. \] The curve is a density; probability is literally the area.

(c) Using the fact that total area is 1

Total area under the curve is 1. We are told:

  • Area from 0 to 5 minutes: 0.2,
  • Area from 5 to 10 minutes: 0.6.

So the area from 0 to 10 minutes is $0.2 + 0.6 = 0.8$. The area from 10 to 20 minutes must be \[ 1 - 0.8 = 0.2. \] That area is $P(10 < T \le 20)$.

So $P(10 < T \le 20) = 0.2$.

Problem 2: Daily Study Time as a Continuous Model

Suppose the number of hours a randomly chosen student studies in a day is modeled by a continuous distribution with mean $\mu = 2.0$ hours and standard deviation $\sigma = 0.5$ hours.

  1. In words, what does the mean $\mu = 2.0$ hours tell you about this distribution?
  2. In words, what does the standard deviation $\sigma = 0.5$ hours tell you?
  3. Consider another course where daily study time has the same mean $\mu = 2.0$ hours but a larger standard deviation $\sigma = 1.0$ hours. How would the shape of that distribution compare to the first one?
Show solution

(a) Interpreting the mean

The mean $\mu = 2.0$ hours is the long-run average study time per day. If we look across all students over many days, their study times would balance at about 2 hours. It is the “center” of the distribution.

(b) Interpreting the standard deviation

The standard deviation $\sigma = 0.5$ hours measures how spread out the study times are around the mean. A standard deviation of 0.5 hour means that a typical daily study time is about half an hour away from the mean of 2 hours. Most students are between about 1.5 and 2.5 hours.

(c) Same mean, larger standard deviation

If another course has the same mean $\mu = 2.0$ but a larger standard deviation $\sigma = 1.0$, then the second distribution is more spread out. The curves would be:

  • First course: narrow, taller bump around 2 hours (study times tightly clustered).
  • Second course: wider, flatter bump around 2 hours (more variation; some students study much less or much more).

The center (mean) is the same, but the spread is different.

Problem 3: Number of Emails to a Club Advisor

Let $E$ be the number of emails a student club advisor receives from club members in a randomly chosen day. The advisor believes the long-run behavior is described by this discrete probability distribution:

$e$ (emails) 01234
$P(E=e)$ 0.200.350.250.150.05
  1. Check that this is a valid probability distribution.
  2. Find $P(E \ge 2)$ and interpret it in context.
  3. Compute the mean number of emails per day, $\mu_E = \sum e \cdot P(E=e)$.
  4. Interpret $\mu_E$ in a sentence that does not use symbols.
Show solution

(a) Valid distribution?

Add the probabilities: \[ 0.20 + 0.35 + 0.25 + 0.15 + 0.05 = 1.00. \] All probabilities are between 0 and 1 and the total is 1, so this is a valid probability distribution.

(b) $P(E \ge 2)$

\[ P(E \ge 2) = P(E=2) + P(E=3) + P(E=4) = 0.25 + 0.15 + 0.05 = 0.45. \] So on a randomly chosen day, there is a 45% chance that the advisor receives 2 or more emails.

(c) Mean number of emails

\[ \begin{aligned} \mu_E &= 0\cdot 0.20 + 1\cdot 0.35 + 2\cdot 0.25 + 3\cdot 0.15 + 4\cdot 0.05 \\ &= 0 + 0.35 + 0.50 + 0.45 + 0.20 \\ &= 1.50. \end{aligned} \]

(d) Interpretation of $\mu_E$

On average, the advisor receives about 1.5 emails per day from club members. In the long run, if we look at many days, the mean number of emails per day will be about 1.5.

Problem 4: Sample vs Model for Exercise Days

A health center models the number of days per week that students get at least 30 minutes of exercise with this discrete distribution for $D$:

$d$ (days/week) 01234567
$P(D=d)$ 0.050.100.200.25 0.150.100.080.07

Now suppose we take a sample of 12 students and record their number of exercise days:

$3,\ 3,\ 2,\ 4,\ 0,\ 1,\ 5,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 6,\ 2$.

  1. Construct a frequency table and relative-frequency table for this sample.
  2. Compute the sample mean $\bar d$ for these 12 students.
  3. Suppose the population mean from the model above is $\mu_D = 3.2$ days. Compare $\bar d$ and $\mu_D$. Are they the same? Should we expect them to be?
  4. In words, explain what sampling variability means in this context.
Show solution

(a) Frequency and relative-frequency tables

First count how many times each value appears in the sample of 12:

  • $0$: once (one student),
  • $1$: once,
  • $2$: three times,
  • $3$: three times,
  • $4$: twice,
  • $5$: once,
  • $6$: once,
  • $7$: zero times.
$d$ 01234567
Frequency 11332110
Rel. freq. 1/121/123/123/122/121/121/120

In decimals, that’s approximately:

  • $0$: $0.083$,
  • $1$: $0.083$,
  • $2$: $0.25$,
  • $3$: $0.25$,
  • $4$: $0.167$,
  • $5$: $0.083$,
  • $6$: $0.083$,
  • $7$: $0$.

(b) Sample mean

Add up the 12 values: \[ 3 + 3 + 2 + 4 + 0 + 1 + 5 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 6 + 2 = 35. \] With $n = 12$ students: \[ \bar d = \frac{35}{12} \approx 2.92 \text{ days}. \]

(c) Comparing $\bar d$ and $\mu_D$

We are told the population model has mean $\mu_D = 3.2$ days. Our sample mean is $\bar d \approx 2.92$ days. They are not exactly the same, but they are reasonably close.

We do not expect $\bar d$ to equal $\mu_D$ exactly. Every random sample gives slightly different data and therefore a slightly different sample mean.

(d) Sampling variability

Sampling variability means that statistics like $\bar d$ will change from sample to sample, even when the population model is fixed. If we took another random sample of 12 students, we would almost surely get different exercise counts and a different sample mean.

The population mean $\mu_D$ is a fixed (but often unknown) number. The sample mean $\bar d$ is random because it depends on which students happen to be in the sample.
