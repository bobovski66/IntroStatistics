Math 146 — Week 4 Prequiz Practice

Normal distributions, z-scores, and the Central Limit Theorem. Expand each solution to check your work.

Reference Formulas

For \(X \sim N(\mu,\,\sigma)\): \(\displaystyle z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\)

For a sample mean: \(\bar{X} \approx N\!\left(\mu,\,\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\), \(\displaystyle z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\)

\(z\)-table gives \(P(Z \le z)\).  Right tail: \(P(Z > z) = 1 - P(Z \le z)\).  Middle: \(P(a \le Z \le b) = P(Z \le b) - P(Z \le a)\).

Problem 1 — General Normal Distribution

Adult heights are approximately normal with mean 67 inches and standard deviation 3 inches. Let \(X \sim N(67,\,3)\).

  1. Find the \(z\)-score for a height of 70 inches.
  2. Find \(P(X \le 70)\) using the \(z\)-table.
  3. Sketch the normal curve, mark the mean, mark \(x = 70\), and shade the region for \(P(X \le 70)\).
Show Solution
  1. \[ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} = \frac{70 - 67}{3} = \frac{3}{3} = 1.00 \]
    Answer: \(z = 1.00\)
  2. From the \(z\)-table, \(P(Z \le 1.00) = 0.8413\).
    Answer: \(P(X \le 70) \approx 0.8413\)

  3. The shaded region is the left 84.13% of the curve. The dashed vertical line marks \(z = 1.00\), which corresponds to \(x = 70\) inches.

Problem 2 — Probability Between Two Values

A machine fills bottles with amounts that follow \(X \sim N(16.0,\;0.3)\) ounces.

  1. Find the \(z\)-scores for 15.8 ounces and 16.4 ounces.
  2. Find \(P(15.8 \le X \le 16.4)\).
  3. Sketch the normal curve and shade the region that matches your probability.
Show Solution
  1. \[ z_{15.8} = \frac{15.8 - 16.0}{0.3} = \frac{-0.2}{0.3} \approx -0.67 \] \[ z_{16.4} = \frac{16.4 - 16.0}{0.3} = \frac{0.4}{0.3} \approx 1.33 \]
    Answer: \(z = -0.67\) for 15.8 oz; \(\;z = 1.33\) for 16.4 oz.
  2. \[ P(15.8 \le X \le 16.4) = P(-0.67 \le Z \le 1.33) = P(Z \le 1.33) - P(Z \le -0.67) \] \[ = 0.9082 - 0.2514 = 0.6568 \]
    Answer: \(P(15.8 \le X \le 16.4) \approx 0.6568\)

  3. The shaded band is not symmetric: 16.4 oz is 1.33 standard deviations above the mean, while 15.8 oz is only 0.67 below, so the right boundary is farther out.

Problem 3 — Sampling Distribution and Standard Error

A population has mean \(\mu = 50\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 20\). Samples of size \(n = 25\) are taken.

  1. What is the mean of the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\)?
  2. What is the standard error of \(\bar{X}\)?
  3. Write the approximate sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) in the form \(\bar{X} \approx N(\text{mean},\;\text{standard error})\).
Show Solution
  1. The mean of the sampling distribution always equals the population mean.
    Answer: \(\mu_{\bar{X}} = \mu = 50\)
  2. \[ \text{SE} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{20}{\sqrt{25}} = \frac{20}{5} = 4 \]
    Answer: Standard error \(= 4\)
  3. Answer: \(\bar{X} \approx N(50,\;4)\)

    By the Central Limit Theorem, sample means are approximately normally distributed for \(n \ge 25\). Averaging over 25 observations reduces the spread from \(\sigma = 20\) to \(\text{SE} = 4\). The center does not move — the mean of \(\bar{X}\) is still \(\mu\).

Problem 4 — CLT Probability for a Sample Mean

The lifetime of a battery has mean 500 hours and standard deviation 40 hours. A random sample of \(n = 64\) batteries is taken.

  1. What is the standard error of the sample mean lifetime?
  2. Find the \(z\)-score for \(\bar{x} = 495\).
  3. Find \(P(\bar{X} \le 495)\).
Show Solution
  1. \[ \text{SE} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{40}{\sqrt{64}} = \frac{40}{8} = 5 \]
    Answer: Standard error \(= 5\) hours
  2. \[ z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{495 - 500}{5} = \frac{-5}{5} = -1.00 \]
    Answer: \(z = -1.00\)
  3. From the \(z\)-table, \(P(Z \le {-1.00}) = 0.1587\).
    Answer: \(P(\bar{X} \le 495) \approx 0.1587\)

    A sample mean of 495 hours is exactly 1 standard error below \(\mu = 500\). There is about a 15.9% chance of seeing a result this low or lower, which is not unusual.

What to Watch For

z-score formula left tail right tail middle area standard error CLT sampling distribution
  • The \(z\)-table always gives \(P(Z \le z)\) — a left-tail area.
  • For a middle region, subtract two left-tail values: \(P(a \le Z \le b) = P(Z \le b) - P(Z \le a)\).
  • The standard error is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), not \(\sigma\). Using the wrong denominator is the most common mistake here.
  • The sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) is centered at \(\mu\) — sampling does not shift the mean, only tighten the spread.
  • Larger \(n\) gives a smaller SE and a tighter, taller sampling distribution.
