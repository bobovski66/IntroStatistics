Purpose of the lab

This lab uses first-quarter fireball report data from the American Meteor Society (AMS). The 2026 data are compared with first-quarter data from 2021 through 2025. The goal is to apply standard two-sample inference methods, then decide what they tell you about how 2026 differs from the earlier years.

Each row in the dataset is one fireball event. The variable you will analyze is reports_count, the number of eyewitness reports submitted for that event. You will run two-sample $z$-procedures — one for means and one for proportions. Both are applied to the same 261 events, but they summarize the data in different ways, and that difference is the point of the lab.

Interpreting the statistics: The sample size records the number of events in each group. The mean and median both describe center; a substantial difference between them is evidence of skewness. Part 3. Confidence interval for the difference in means

Next, estimate the difference between the 2026 population mean and the baseline population mean number of reports per event. A confidence interval gives a range of plausible values for this difference, based on the sample data.

Use of the normal approximation: The individual reports_count values are skewed. However, by the Central Limit Theorem, the difference of two sample means is approximately normal when both sample sizes are large. The sample sizes in this lab are 194 and 67, so the normal approximation is reasonable for the difference of means. The standard error measures the estimated sampling variability of the difference in sample means.
\[ (\bar{x}_{2026} - \bar{x}_{\text{base}}) \;\pm\; z^{*} \sqrt{\frac{s_{2026}^{2}}{n_{2026}} + \frac{s_{\text{base}}^{2}}{n_{\text{base}}}} \] Two-sample $z$ confidence interval for $\mu_{2026} - \mu_{\text{base}}$

For a 95% confidence interval, use $z^{*} = 1.96$. Since the population standard deviations are unknown, substitute the sample standard deviations from Part 1.

Part 4. Hypothesis test for the difference in means

The confidence interval estimates the true difference in population means. The hypothesis test addresses the related question of whether the observed difference is statistically significant. Under the null hypothesis, the two population means are equal, so the difference is zero. The test statistic measures the observed difference in standard-error units.

Predict first: what controls the size of the $z$-statistic?

Before you read on, look at the formula below and try to answer in your head: the two sample means clearly differ. What would have to be true about the standard error for that difference to still produce a small $z$? Then open the explanation.

A hypothesis test does not just ask whether the two sample means differ; it compares that difference with the standard error. If the observed difference is large relative to the standard error, the $z$-statistic is large in magnitude. If the standard error is large, the same observed difference produces a smaller $z$-statistic. Because the standard error depends on the sample standard deviations, unusually large spread in either group can shrink $z$ even when the means look far apart.

\[ z \;=\; \dfrac{(\bar{x}_{2026} - \bar{x}_{\text{base}}) - 0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{s_{2026}^{2}}{n_{2026}} + \dfrac{s_{\text{base}}^{2}}{n_{\text{base}}}}} \] Two-sample $z$-statistic for the difference in means
Step 1: State.
$H_0 : \mu_{2026} = \mu_{\text{base}}$
$H_a : \mu_{2026} \neq \mu_{\text{base}}$
Use $\alpha = 0.05$.

Compute the $z$-statistic, look up the two-sided $p$-value in your normal table or use technology, and write your decision.

Part 5. Examining the standard error

The mean comparison may show a noticeable difference between the two sample means while still failing to reject the null hypothesis. To understand this result, take the standard error apart and see which group contributes most to it.

\[ \text{SE} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{s_{2026}^{2}}{n_{2026}} + \frac{s_{\text{base}}^{2}}{n_{\text{base}}}} \] Standard error of $\bar{x}_{2026} - \bar{x}_{\text{base}}$

Compute the ratio of standard deviations and the two terms inside the radical separately. These quantities show how much each group contributes to the standard error. Look at your two terms before reading the explanation: which group dominates, and can you say why?

Check your reasoning

The test does not only ask whether the two sample means differ. It asks whether the difference is large relative to the expected sampling variability of that difference, which the standard error summarizes. The standard error depends on the sample standard deviation of each group, and a single extreme observation inflates a sample standard deviation. So one unusually large 2026 event increases $s_{2026}$, which enlarges the $s_{2026}^{2}/n_{2026}$ term, raises the standard error, shrinks the magnitude of $z$, and pushes the $p$-value up. That chain is how a real difference in the means can still fail to reach significance.

Part 6. Comparing proportions of large events

The mean of reports_count is affected by very large observations. A related question is to compare the fraction of events that exceed a specified threshold. This changes the statistic from an average report count to a proportion of large events.

Changing the statistic: With the threshold reports_count ≥ 50, an event with 3,229 reports and an event with 51 reports each count as exactly one large event. The proportion records only whether an event clears the threshold, not by how much. This is what makes a proportion far less sensitive than a mean to a single extreme observation.
