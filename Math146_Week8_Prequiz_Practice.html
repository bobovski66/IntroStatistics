Math 146 — Week 8 Prequiz Practice

Chi-squared tests for categorical data. Expand each solution to check your work.

Reference Formulas

Chi-squared statistic: \[\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\]

Expected count (equal proportions): \(E = n/k\)

Expected count (independence): \[\displaystyle E = \frac{(\text{row total})(\text{column total})}{\text{grand total}}\]

Degrees of freedom: goodness-of-fit \(df = k-1\)  ·  independence \(df=(r-1)(c-1)\)

Problem 1 — Goodness-of-Fit: Lunch Choices

A campus survey asks 100 students which lunch option they prefer. The null hypothesis says all four options are equally popular.

Lunch choicePizzaSandwichesSaladTacos
Observed \(O\)40221820
  1. What is the expected count for each lunch choice?
  2. Fill in the expected counts and chi-squared contributions.
  3. Add the four contributions to find \(\chi^2\).
  4. Find the degrees of freedom.
  5. In one sentence, explain what a large chi-squared statistic means in this setting.
Show Solution
  1. Under \(H_0\) all categories are equally likely, so \(E = 100/4 = 25\) for each.
  2. Category\(O\)\(E\)\((O-E)^2/E\)
    Pizza4025\((40-25)^2/25 = 225/25 = 9.00\)
    Sandwiches2225\((22-25)^2/25 = 9/25 = 0.36\)
    Salad1825\((18-25)^2/25 = 49/25 = 1.96\)
    Tacos2025\((20-25)^2/25 = 25/25 = 1.00\)
  3. \[\chi^2 = 9.00 + 0.36 + 1.96 + 1.00 = 12.32\]
    Answer: \(\chi^2 = 12.32\)
  4. \(df = k - 1 = 4 - 1 = 3\)
    Answer: \(df = 3\)
  5. Answer: A large chi-squared statistic means the observed counts are far from the expected counts, providing evidence against the null hypothesis that all four lunch options are equally popular.

Pizza is the only category with a large contribution (9.00) — it drives most of the test statistic. Sandwiches, Salad, and Tacos are much closer to their expected values.

Problem 2 — Why Do We Divide by the Expected Count?

Both cases have a raw difference of 5.

CaseObserved \(O\)Expected \(E\)\(|O-E|\)
A15105
B1051005
  1. Compute the contribution for Case A: \((15-10)^2/10\).
  2. Compute the contribution for Case B: \((105-100)^2/100\).
  3. Explain why the same raw difference can have a different chi-squared contribution.
Show Solution
  1. \((15-10)^2/10 = 25/10 =\)
    Answer: 2.5
  2. \((105-100)^2/100 = 25/100 =\)
    Answer: 0.25
  3. Answer: Dividing by \(E\) scales the squared difference relative to the baseline expectation. A discrepancy of 5 when you expected 10 is a 50% deviation — very surprising. The same discrepancy of 5 when you expected 100 is only a 5% deviation — quite ordinary. The chi-squared contribution measures relative deviation, not absolute deviation.

    This is why the formula uses \((O-E)^2/E\) rather than just \((O-E)^2\). Without dividing by \(E\), categories with large expected counts would dominate the statistic regardless of how proportionally close the observed and expected values are.

Problem 3 — Expected Counts for Independence

A class studies whether preferred study method is related to exam result.

Study methodPassedDid not passRow total
Flashcards181230
Practice problems42850
Videos201030
Column total8030110
  1. Find \(E\) for Flashcards / Passed: \((30)(80)/110\).
  2. Find \(E\) for Flashcards / Did not pass: \((30)(30)/110\).
  3. Find \(E\) for Practice problems / Passed: \((50)(80)/110\).
  4. Find the degrees of freedom for this \(3 \times 2\) table.
Show Solution
  1. \(E = (30)(80)/110 = 2400/110 \approx\)
    Answer: \(E \approx 21.82\)
  2. \(E = (30)(30)/110 = 900/110 \approx\)
    Answer: \(E \approx 8.18\)
  3. \(E = (50)(80)/110 = 4000/110 \approx\)
    Answer: \(E \approx 36.36\)
  4. \(df = (r-1)(c-1) = (3-1)(2-1) = 2 \times 1 =\)
    Answer: \(df = 2\)

Note that the expected counts in each row sum to the row total (e.g., \(21.82 + 8.18 = 30\)), and each column's expected counts sum to the column total. This is a useful check.

Problem 4 — Decision and Conclusion

A chi-squared test of independence gives \(\chi^2 = 8.40\), \(df = 2\), \(p\text{-value} = 0.015\). Use \(\alpha = 0.05\).

  1. Should we reject or fail to reject \(H_0\)?
  2. Write a conclusion in context for the study-method example. Use the words evidence and associated.
Show Solution
  1. Since \(p\text{-value} = 0.015 < \alpha = 0.05\):
    Reject \(H_0\).
  2. Answer: There is sufficient evidence at \(\alpha = 0.05\) to conclude that study method and exam result are associated — the data do not support independence between how students study and whether they pass.

    The conclusion avoids saying one variable causes the other. Chi-squared tests establish association, not causation. Also note: we never say "accept \(H_0\)" — we either reject it or fail to reject it.

What to Watch For

expected counts chi-squared formula degrees of freedom why divide by \(E\) independence vs. causation conclusion language
  • For goodness-of-fit with equal proportions, \(E = n/k\) — every category gets the same expected count.
  • For independence, each cell gets its own \(E\) computed from the marginal totals: \(E = (\text{row total})(\text{column total})/\text{grand total}\).
  • Degrees of freedom: goodness-of-fit \(df = k-1\); independence \(df = (r-1)(c-1)\). A \(4\times 1\) table has \(df=3\); a \(3\times 2\) table has \(df=2\).
  • The chi-squared statistic measures total relative discrepancy. Dividing by \(E\) ensures a difference of 5 is not treated the same whether \(E=10\) or \(E=100\).
  • Conclusions must say associated (or not independent), not causes.
