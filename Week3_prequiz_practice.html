Reference Formulas

\[ \text{z-table gives } P(Z \le z) \]

\[ P(Z > z)=1-P(Z \le z), \qquad P(a \le Z \le b)=P(Z \le b)-P(Z \le a) \]

Problem 1. Uniform Distribution

A local coffee shop fills cups with a uniformly distributed amount of espresso between \(1.5\) and \(2.5\) ounces. Let \(X\) be the amount poured.

  1. What is the height of the density curve? Verify that the total area equals \(1\).
  2. Sketch the density curve on the axes below. Label both axes, including the height.
  3. Find \(P(X<2.0)\) by computing a rectangle area. Shade the region on your sketch above.
  4. Find \(\mu\). Interpret this value in one sentence.
Show full solution
  1. For a uniform distribution on \([a,b]\), the height is \[ \frac{1}{b-a}. \] Here \(a=1.5\) and \(b=2.5\), so \[ \text{height}=\frac{1}{2.5-1.5}=\frac{1}{1}=1. \] The width of the interval is \(2.5-1.5=1\), so the total area is \[ (\text{height})(\text{width})=(1)(1)=1. \]
    Answer: The height is \(\boxed{1}\), and the total area is \(\boxed{1}\).
  2. The density curve is a horizontal line at height \(1\) from \(x=1.5\) to \(x=2.5\). The axes should be labeled \(x\) for ounces poured and \(f(x)\) for density.
  3. Since the distribution is uniform, the probability is the shaded rectangle area from \(1.5\) to \(2.0\): \[ P(X<2.0)=\frac{2.0-1.5}{2.5-1.5}=\frac{0.5}{1}=0.5. \] Since the density is continuous, \(P(X<2.0)=P(X\le 2.0)\).
    Answer: \(\boxed{P(X<2.0)=0.5}\).
  4. For a uniform distribution, \[ \mu=\frac{a+b}{2}. \] So \[ \mu=\frac{1.5+2.5}{2}=\frac{4.0}{2}=2.0. \]
    Answer: \(\boxed{\mu=2.0}\) ounces.
    Interpretation: over many pours, the average amount poured would be \(2.0\) ounces.

Problem 2. Standard Normal: Empirical Rule

For the standard normal distribution \(Z \sim N(0,1)\), use the Empirical Rule. Do not use the z-table for this problem.

  1. What percentage of the distribution lies between \(z=-2\) and \(z=2\)?
  2. What percentage lies above \(z=1\)?
  3. Draw and label the standard normal curve. Shade the region representing \(P(Z>1)\) and label it with the probability.
Show full solution
  1. By the Empirical Rule, about \(95\%\) of the distribution lies within \(2\) standard deviations of the mean. That means \[ P(-2 \le Z \le 2)\approx 0.95. \]
    Answer: \(\boxed{95\%}\).
  2. By the Empirical Rule, about \(68\%\) lies between \(-1\) and \(1\). Since the curve is symmetric, half of that \(68\%\) lies between \(0\) and \(1\), which is \(34\%\). Also, half the total area lies to the right of \(0\), so \[ P(Z>1)=0.50-0.34=0.16. \]
    Answer: \(\boxed{16\%}\) or \(\boxed{0.16}\).
  3. The shaded region to the right of \(z=1\) has probability about \(0.16\).

Problem 3. Using the z-Table

For each part, sketch the normal curve, shade the correct region, and then use the z-table to find the probability.

  1. \(P(Z \le 1.72)\)
  2. \(P(Z > -0.63)\)
  3. \(P(-1.20 \le Z \le 0.50)\)
Show full solution
  1. We want the area to the left of \(z=1.72\), so this is a direct table lookup. Using the row \(1.7\) and the column \(0.02\), \[ P(Z \le 1.72)=0.9573. \]
    Answer: \(\boxed{P(Z \le 1.72)=0.9573}\).
  2. We want the area to the right of \(-0.63\): \[ P(Z>-0.63)=1-P(Z\le -0.63). \] From the z-table, \[ P(Z\le -0.63)=0.2643. \] So \[ P(Z>-0.63)=1-0.2643=0.7357. \]
    Answer: \(\boxed{P(Z>-0.63)=0.7357}\).
  3. We want the area between \(-1.20\) and \(0.50\), so subtract two left-tail areas: \[ P(-1.20 \le Z \le 0.50)=P(Z\le 0.50)-P(Z\le -1.20). \] From the z-table, \[ P(Z\le 0.50)=0.6915 \qquad\text{and}\qquad P(Z\le -1.20)=0.1151. \] Therefore, \[ P(-1.20 \le Z \le 0.50)=0.6915-0.1151=0.5764. \]
    Answer: \(\boxed{P(-1.20 \le Z \le 0.50)=0.5764}\).

I double-checked the z-table values and arithmetic: \(P(Z\le 1.72)=0.9573\), \(P(Z\le -0.63)=0.2643\), \(P(Z\le 0.50)=0.6915\), \(P(Z\le -1.20)=0.1151\), and \(0.6915-0.1151=0.5764\).

What to Watch For

uniform distribution density as area empirical rule left tail right tail middle area
  • For a uniform distribution, probability is just area of a rectangle.
  • On a continuous distribution, probability comes from area, not bar height.
  • The z-table always gives left-tail area \(P(Z \le z)\).
  • Right-tail problems need subtraction from \(1\).
  • Middle-area problems need subtraction of two left-tail values.
