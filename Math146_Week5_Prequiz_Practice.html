Math 146 — Week 5 Prequiz Practice

Confidence intervals for means and proportions, margin of error, and required sample size. Expand each solution to check your work.

Reference Formulas

Confidence interval for a mean (\(\sigma\) known): \[ \bar{x} \pm z^* \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Common critical values: \(90\%: z^* = 1.645\), \(\quad 95\%: z^* = 1.96\), \(\quad 99\%: z^* = 2.576\)

Confidence interval for a proportion: \[ \hat{p} \pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \]

Required sample size for a mean: \[ n \ge \left(\frac{z^* \sigma}{E}\right)^2 \] where \(E\) is the desired margin of error.

Problem 1 — Confidence Interval for a Population Mean

A school counselor takes a random sample of 36 students and finds a mean sleep time of \(\bar{x} = 6.8\) hours per night. Assume the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 1.5\) hours.

  1. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean sleep time \(\mu\).
  2. State the margin of error.
  3. Write one sentence interpreting your interval in context.
Show Solution
  1. For 95% confidence, \(z^* = 1.96\). The standard error is: \[ \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1.5}{\sqrt{36}} = \frac{1.5}{6} = 0.25 \] The margin of error is: \[ E = z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \times 0.25 = 0.49 \] The confidence interval is: \[ \bar{x} \pm E = 6.8 \pm 0.49 \] \[ (6.8 - 0.49,\; 6.8 + 0.49) = (6.31,\; 7.29) \]
    Answer: \((6.31,\; 7.29)\) hours
  2. Answer: The margin of error is \(E = 0.49\) hours.
  3. Answer: We are 95% confident that the true mean nightly sleep time for students at this school is between 6.31 and 7.29 hours.

    The phrase "95% confident" means that if we repeated this sampling process many times, about 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true population mean \(\mu\). It does not mean there is a 95% chance \(\mu\) is in this particular interval — \(\mu\) is a fixed (unknown) value, not a random one.

Problem 2 — Confidence Level and Width

A random sample of 64 packages has mean weight \(\bar{x} = 12.4\) ounces, with known population standard deviation \(\sigma = 1.2\) ounces.

  1. Construct a 90% confidence interval for \(\mu\).
  2. Construct a 99% confidence interval for \(\mu\).
  3. Which interval is wider? Explain briefly why.
Show Solution
  1. The standard error is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 1.2/\sqrt{64} = 1.2/8 = 0.15\). For 90% confidence, \(z^* = 1.645\): \[ E = 1.645 \times 0.15 = 0.247 \] \[ \bar{x} \pm E = 12.4 \pm 0.247 \]
    Answer: \((12.153,\; 12.647)\) ounces
  2. For 99% confidence, \(z^* = 2.576\): \[ E = 2.576 \times 0.15 = 0.386 \] \[ \bar{x} \pm E = 12.4 \pm 0.386 \]
    Answer: \((12.014,\; 12.786)\) ounces
  3. Confidence level\(z^*\)Margin of errorInterval
    90%1.6450.247(12.153, 12.647)
    99%2.5760.386(12.014, 12.786)
    Answer: The 99% interval is wider. A higher confidence level requires a larger critical value \(z^*\), which increases the margin of error and stretches the interval. To be more confident that you have captured \(\mu\), you must cast a wider net.

Problem 3 — Required Sample Size for a Mean

A transportation planner wants to estimate the mean commute time for workers in a city with a margin of error no more than 2 minutes at 95% confidence. Historical data suggest \(\sigma = 10\) minutes.

  1. Find the required sample size.
  2. Why do we round this answer up rather than down?
Show Solution
  1. For 95% confidence, \(z^* = 1.96\), and we want \(E = 2\): \[ n \ge \left(\frac{z^* \sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96 \times 10}{2}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{19.6}{2}\right)^2 = (9.8)^2 = 96.04 \]
    Answer: \(n \ge 97\) students
  2. Answer: We round up because rounding down to 96 would give a margin of error slightly larger than 2 minutes, failing to meet the stated requirement. We need at least 97 observations to guarantee the margin of error is no more than 2 minutes.

    The formula gives the minimum real-number value of \(n\). Since sample sizes must be whole numbers, any non-integer result is always rounded up to the next integer, even if the decimal part is very small (e.g., 96.01 rounds up to 97).

Problem 4 — Confidence Interval for a Population Proportion

In a survey of 250 adults, 145 say they check the weather forecast on their phone every day.

  1. Compute the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\).
  2. Check the success-failure condition.
  3. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true population proportion \(p\).
  4. Write one sentence interpreting your interval in context.
Show Solution
  1. \[ \hat{p} = \frac{145}{250} = 0.58 \]
    Answer: \(\hat{p} = 0.58\)
  2. The success-failure condition requires both \(n\hat{p} \ge 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \ge 10\): \[ n\hat{p} = 250 \times 0.58 = 145 \ge 10 \quad \checkmark \] \[ n(1-\hat{p}) = 250 \times 0.42 = 105 \ge 10 \quad \checkmark \]
    Answer: Both conditions are satisfied, so the normal approximation is valid and we may proceed.
  3. The standard error for a proportion is: \[ \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.58 \times 0.42}{250}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.2436}{250}} = \sqrt{0.0009744} \approx 0.0312 \] For 95% confidence, \(z^* = 1.96\): \[ E = 1.96 \times 0.0312 \approx 0.0612 \] \[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.58 \pm 0.0612 \]
    Answer: \((0.519,\; 0.641)\)
  4. Answer: We are 95% confident that the true proportion of adults who check the weather forecast on their phone every day is between 51.9% and 64.1%.

    Notice that the entire interval lies above 50%, so the data provide reasonably strong evidence that a majority of adults in the population check the weather daily.

What to Watch For

critical value margin of error standard error confidence level vs. width sample size success-failure condition interpreting intervals
  • The standard error for a mean is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), not \(\sigma\). Forgetting the square root is the most common arithmetic error here.
  • Higher confidence level \(\Rightarrow\) larger \(z^*\) \(\Rightarrow\) wider interval. There is always a trade-off between precision and confidence.
  • For required sample size, always round up, never down — even if the decimal is very small.
  • Before computing a proportion interval, verify the success-failure condition: both \(n\hat{p} \ge 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \ge 10\) must hold.
  • An interpretation sentence must reference the specific context (sleep hours, package weight, etc.) and state the confidence level — not just quote the numbers.
