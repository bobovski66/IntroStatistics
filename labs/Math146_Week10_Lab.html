Objectives

  • Compute a least-squares regression line from summary statistics using \(b=r\,s_y/s_x\) and \(a=\bar{y}-b\bar{x}\).
  • Use a regression line to make a prediction and interpret the slope in context.
  • Compute \(r^2\) and interpret it as the proportion of variability in \(y\) explained by \(x\).
  • Find a regression line from raw bivariate data and recognize the limits of correlation and extrapolation.

Reference Formulas

Regression slope and intercept: \[b=r\cdot\frac{s_y}{s_x} \qquad a=\bar{y}-b\bar{x} \qquad \hat{y}=a+bx\]

Residual: \(e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i\)  ·  \(r^2\) = proportion of variability in \(y\) explained by the linear relationship with \(x\).

The regression line always passes through \((\bar{x},\,\bar{y})\).

\(r^2=0.7225\) (72.25%).

\(r^2=0.85^2=0.7225\): 72.25% of the variability in improvement score is explained by the linear relationship with weeks of therapy. The remaining 27.75% reflects other factors — individual healing rates, therapy intensity, injury type — not captured by this simple linear model.

(d) The residual plot shows a curved pattern. Is the linear model appropriate? What might fit better?
Show solution

A curved residual pattern means the linear model is not appropriate.

Residuals should look randomly scattered around zero with no pattern if a linear model fits well. A curve suggests a nonlinear relationship — perhaps improvement levels off over time — and a curved model (such as logarithmic or quadratic) might fit better.

Problem 2 — Interpreting Regression in Context

A dataset of 50 cities records average summer temperature (\(x\), °F) and annual ice cream sales per capita (\(y\), dollars). Regression line: \(\hat{y}=-120+4.5x\), \(r^2=0.61\). The warmest city in the data has \(x=95\)°F.
(a) Interpret the slope in context: what does 4.5 mean for a real city?
Show solution

Each additional degree of average summer temperature predicts about \$4.50 more in ice cream sales per capita.

The slope is a rate of change: for every 1°F increase in average summer temperature, predicted per-capita ice cream sales rise by \$4.50, on average.

(b) Interpret \(r^2=0.61\) in context: what percentage of variability is explained? What explains the rest?
Show solution

61% of the variability in ice cream sales is explained by average summer temperature.

The remaining 39% comes from other factors — local prices, population demographics, availability of ice cream shops, and so on — that aren't captured by temperature alone.

(c) Is predicting at \(x=110\)°F appropriate? Use the word extrapolation.
Show solution

No — this is extrapolation.

The warmest city in the data only reaches 95°F, so predicting at 110°F means applying the line far outside the range of observed data. There's no evidence the linear relationship continues to hold out there.

(d) A journalist says "Eating ice cream raises body temperature." What is wrong? Name the reasoning error and identify the underlying cause.
Show solution

This confuses correlation with causation.

Temperature and ice cream sales move together, but that doesn't mean one causes the other. The underlying cause is a lurking variable — the season — that drives both hot weather and higher ice cream demand at the same time.

Problem 3 — Correlation and r²

Three scatterplots have correlations \(r=-0.92\), \(r=0.45\), \(r=0.01\).
(a) Match each \(r\) to its description: strong negative linear · weak positive linear · no linear relationship.
Show solution

\(r=-0.92\): strong negative. \(r=0.45\): weak positive. \(r=0.01\): no linear relationship.

The sign gives the direction and the magnitude (distance from 0) gives the strength of the linear association.

(b) Compute \(r^2\) for each.
Show solution

\(0.8464\) (84.64%), \(0.2025\) (20.25%), \(0.0001\) (0.01%).

Squaring \(r\) removes the sign, which is why \(r^2\) tells you the strength of the relationship regardless of its direction.

(c) For \(r=0.01\): could \(x\) and \(y\) still be strongly associated? What does \(r\) measure — and what does it miss?
Show solution

Yes — \(r\) only measures linear association.

A strong curved relationship, such as a U-shape or a cycle, can produce \(r\approx0\) even though \(x\) and \(y\) are tightly linked in a nonlinear way. Low \(r\) rules out a strong straight-line pattern, not association in general.
