Objectives

  • Verify that a table is a valid probability distribution and compute its expected value \(E(X)\).
  • Apply the geometric model to find \(P(X=k)\) and the mean number of trials \(\mu=1/p\).
  • Apply the binomial model to compute probabilities, the mean \(\mu=np\), and the standard deviation \(\sigma=\sqrt{np(1-p)}\).
  • Interpret each computed value as a statement about the real-world situation.

Reference Formulas

\[ P(X=k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p \qquad \mu_{\text{geo}} = \frac{1}{p} \]

\[ P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \qquad \mu_{\text{bin}} = np \qquad \sigma_{\text{bin}} = \sqrt{np(1-p)} \]

\[ \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \qquad E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x) \]

Problem 1 — Probability Distribution and Expected Value

A simple game uses a spinner with four possible outcomes. The spinner lands on 1 with probability 0.10, on 2 with probability 0.30, on 3 with probability 0.40, and on 4 with probability 0.20. Treat the spinner value as the random variable \(X\), then check that the probabilities form a valid distribution and compute the long-run average outcome.
(a & b) Complete the table below and verify the sum equals 1.
\(x\)1234Sum
\(P(X=x)\)0.10 0.40
Show solution

\(P(X=2)=0.30\), \(P(X=4)=0.20\); the four probabilities sum to \(1.00\).

Every probability distribution's values must total exactly 1. Since \(0.10+0.40=0.50\) is already assigned, the remaining two probabilities must sum to \(0.50\) as well; here they split as \(0.30\) and \(0.20\).

(c) Sketch the probability histogram: what are the bar heights, and what do the axes represent?
Show solution

Four bars at \(x=1,2,3,4\) with heights \(0.10,0.30,0.40,0.20\).

The horizontal axis lists the possible spinner values; the vertical axis is probability. Each bar's height equals \(P(X=x)\), and since the values sum to 1, the total bar area (each bar has width 1) is also 1.

(d) Compute \(\mu=E(X)\).
Show solution

\(E(X)=2.70\).

\(E(X)=1(0.10)+2(0.30)+3(0.40)+4(0.20)=0.10+0.60+1.20+0.80=2.70\). On average the spinner lands near 2.7, weighted toward the more likely outcomes 2 and 3.

Problem 2 — Geometric Distribution

A student guesses on a sequence of multiple-choice questions. Each question has probability \(p=0.20\) of being answered correctly, and the guesses are independent. Let \(X\) be the question number of the first correct answer. This is a geometric setting because we are counting how long it takes until the first success.
(a) Compute \(P(X=3)\) using \((1-p)^{k-1}\cdot p\) with \(k=3\).
Show solution

\(P(X=3)=0.128\).

\(P(X=3)=(0.80)^2\times0.20=0.64\times0.20=0.128\). Two wrong guesses must happen first, then a correct one on the third try.

(b) Compute \(\mu=1/p\) and interpret it in one sentence.
Show solution

\(\mu=1/0.20=5\).

On average, a student guessing with a 20% success rate needs 5 questions before getting the first one right.

(c) Describe the histogram shape: symmetric, skewed left, or skewed right? Why?
Show solution

Skewed right, with the tallest bar at \(k=1\).

Geometric probabilities \((1-p)^{k-1}p\) are largest at \(k=1\) and shrink every time \(k\) increases, since each extra failure multiplies by another factor of \(1-p<1\). That produces a tall bar near \(k=1\) and a long thin tail stretching to the right.

Problem 3 — Binomial Distribution

A drug is effective for 70% of patients. A doctor prescribes it to 6 patients and records how many of those patients respond to the drug. Let \(X\) be the number of patients for whom the drug is effective, so \(n=6\) and \(p=0.70\). This is a binomial setting because there are a fixed number of independent trials and each trial has the same probability of success.
(a) Identify \(n\) and \(p\); list the four binomial conditions and confirm each applies here.
Show solution

\(n=6\), \(p=0.70\); all four binomial conditions hold.

A binomial setting needs: (1) a fixed number of trials, here \(n=6\) patients; (2) each trial independent, since one patient's response doesn't affect another's; (3) only two outcomes per trial, effective or not; (4) the same probability of success on every trial, \(p=0.70\).

(b) Compute \(P(X=6)\).
Show solution

\(P(X=6)\approx0.1176\).

\(\binom{6}{6}=1\), so \(P(X=6)=(0.70)^6=0.117649\approx0.1176\). All six patients responding is fairly unlikely even though the drug works most of the time.

(c) Compute \(P(X=4)\); show the combination \(\binom{6}{4}\) first.
Show solution

\(\binom{6}{4}=15\); \(P(X=4)=15(0.2401)(0.09)=0.3241\).

Four patients responding and two not is the single most likely outcome for this drug and sample size, since \(np=4.2\) is close to 4.

(d) Compute \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), and interpret \(\mu\).
Show solution

\(\mu=6(0.70)=4.2\); \(\sigma=\sqrt{6(0.70)(0.30)}=\sqrt{1.26}\approx1.12\).

Across many groups of 6 patients, we'd expect an average of 4.2 to respond to the drug, typically varying by about 1.12 patients from that average.

(e) Describe the expected histogram shape: symmetric, skewed left, or skewed right? Where is the tallest bar? Why?
Show solution

Skewed left, with the tallest bar near \(x=4\).

Since \(p=0.70>0.50\), successes are more common than failures, which pulls most of the outcomes toward the higher end of \(0\) through \(6\) and stretches a shorter tail toward 0. The tallest bar sits at \(x=4\), closest to \(\mu=4.2\).
