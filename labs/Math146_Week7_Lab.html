Objectives

  • Decide whether two samples are independent or paired.
  • Carry out a two-sample \(z\)-test comparing two population means.
  • Construct a confidence interval for the difference of two means, \(\mu_1-\mu_2\).
  • Carry out a two-sample \(z\)-test comparing two population proportions.

Reference Formulas

\[\text{SE}_{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}=\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}} \qquad z=\frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-0}{\text{SE}} \qquad \text{CI: }(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)\pm z^*\,\text{SE}\]

\[\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}=\frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2} \qquad z=\frac{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2}{\sqrt{\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{pool}})(1/n_1+1/n_2)}}\]

Problem 1 — Independent or Paired?

Before choosing a two-sample method, decide whether the two sets of data are independent or paired. Independent samples come from separate groups. Paired data come from matched measurements, such as before-and-after measurements on the same people.
(a) The same 30 students take a short quiz before and after a review session. Paired or independent?
Show solution

Paired.

The same 30 students are measured twice, so each before-score is matched to that same student's after-score.

(b) Two separate class sections use different textbooks and their quiz scores are compared. Paired or independent?
Show solution

Independent.

The two sections are made up of different students, so there's no natural pairing between a score in one section and a score in the other.

(c) Each patient has blood pressure measured before and after taking a medication. Paired or independent?
Show solution

Paired.

Each patient contributes both a before- and an after-measurement, so the two values are naturally linked by patient.

(d) Ballot measure support is surveyed in two different cities. Paired or independent?
Show solution

Independent.

The two cities are separate populations of respondents with no shared individuals, so the samples don't pair up.

Problem 2 — Two-Sample z-Test for Means

A website team compares the average time, in seconds, that users spend on two page layouts. For Layout 1, \(n_1=40\), \(\bar{x}_1=58.2\), and \(\sigma_1=8.0\). For Layout 2, \(n_2=45\), \(\bar{x}_2=54.6\), and \(\sigma_2=7.5\). Test whether users spend more time on Layout 1 than on Layout 2. Use \(H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2\) and \(H_a:\mu_1>\mu_2\). Test at significance level \(\alpha=0.05\).
(a) Find the standard error.
Show solution

SE \(\approx1.688\).

\(\sigma_1^2/n_1=64/40=1.6\), \(\sigma_2^2/n_2=56.25/45=1.25\). \(\text{SE}=\sqrt{2.85}\approx1.688\).

(b) Find the test statistic \(z\).
Show solution

\(z\approx2.13\).

\(z=3.6/1.688\approx2.13\).

(c) Find the \(p\)-value (right-tailed).
Show solution

\(p\approx0.0166\).

\(1-P(Z\le2.13)=1-0.9834=0.0166\).

(d) Compare the \(p\)-value to \(\alpha\) and write a complete conclusion in context.
Show solution

SE\(\approx1.688\), \(z\approx2.13\), \(p\)-value\(=1-0.9834=0.0166<0.05\).

Reject \(H_0\).

Layout 1 has a greater average time on page: there is sufficient evidence at \(\alpha=0.05\) that users spend more time on Layout 1 than on Layout 2.

Problem 3 — Confidence Interval for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\)

Using the same layout data from Problem 2, construct a 95% confidence interval for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\). The interval estimates how much longer, on average, users spend on Layout 1 compared with Layout 2.
(a) Find the margin of error.
Show solution

ME \(\approx3.31\).

\(1.96\times1.688\approx3.308\).

(b) Construct the confidence interval.
Show solution

95% CI: \((0.29,\,6.91)\) seconds.

ME\(=1.96\times1.688\approx3.308\), so the interval is \(3.6\pm3.31=(0.29,6.91)\).

(c) Does the interval contain 0? State whether 0 is in the interval and what that implies about \(\mu_1-\mu_2\).
Show solution

The interval \((0.29,6.91)\) does not contain 0.

Since 0 is excluded, and the whole interval is positive, this is consistent with \(\mu_1>\mu_2\): Layout 1's true mean time on page is plausibly greater than Layout 2's.

Problem 4 — Two-Sample z-Test for Proportions

A school compares participation in an after-school program for two grade levels. In Grade 11, 84 of 150 students participate. In Grade 12, 63 of 140 students participate. Test whether the participation proportions differ between the two grades. Use \(H_0:p_1=p_2\) and \(H_a:p_1\ne p_2\). Test at significance level \(\alpha=0.05\).
(a) Find the sample proportions.
Show solution

\(\hat{p}_1=0.56\), \(\hat{p}_2=0.45\).

\(84/150=0.56\) and \(63/140=0.45\).

(b) Find the pooled proportion.
Show solution

\(\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}\approx0.5069\).

\(147/290\approx0.5069\). Pooling combines both samples into one estimate of the shared proportion assumed under \(H_0\).

(c) Find the test statistic \(z\).
Show solution

\(z\approx1.87\).

SE\(\approx\sqrt{0.2500\times0.01381}\approx0.05876\). \(z=0.11/0.05876\approx1.87\).

(d) Find the \(p\)-value, then write the decision and a complete conclusion in context.
Show solution

\(\hat{p}_1=0.56\), \(\hat{p}_2=0.45\), \(\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}=147/290\approx0.5069\). SE\(\approx\sqrt{0.2500\times0.01381}\approx0.05876\). \(z=0.11/0.05876\approx1.87\). \(p\)-value\(=2(1-0.9693)=0.0614>0.05\).

Fail to reject \(H_0\).

There is insufficient evidence at \(\alpha=0.05\) that participation rates differ between Grade 11 and Grade 12.
