Objectives

  • Write null and alternative hypotheses and classify a test as left-, right-, or two-tailed.
  • Carry out a one-sample \(z\)-test for a population mean and reach a decision.
  • Interpret a \(p\)-value and compare it to a significance level \(\alpha\).
  • Carry out a one-sample \(z\)-test for a population proportion.

Reference Formulas

Test statistic for a mean: \[\displaystyle z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]

Test statistic for a proportion: \[\displaystyle z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\]

Left-tailed \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\le z)\)   Right-tailed: \(1-P(Z\le z)\)   Two-tailed: \(2(1-P(Z\le|z|))\)

Problem 1 — Writing Hypotheses

For each situation, write the null and alternative hypotheses and identify whether the test is left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed. Pay close attention to the wording of the claim: phrases such as less, higher, and changed determine the direction of \(H_a\).
(a) Cereal boxes are labeled as containing 18 oz on average. A consumer group suspects the true mean fill amount is less than 18 oz. State \(H_0\), \(H_a\), and the test type.
Show solution

\(H_0:\mu=18\), \(H_a:\mu<18\); left-tailed.

"Less than" points the alternative hypothesis toward smaller values, which makes the rejection region sit in the left tail.

(b) A school reports that the average commute time is 24 minutes. A researcher wants to know whether the true mean commute time has changed. State \(H_0\), \(H_a\), and the test type.
Show solution

\(H_0:\mu=24\), \(H_a:\mu\ne24\); two-tailed.

"Changed" allows the true mean to differ in either direction, so both tails count as evidence against \(H_0\).

(c) A website reports that 70% of users finish registration. A manager believes the true completion proportion is higher than 70%. State \(H_0\), \(H_a\), and the test type.
Show solution

\(H_0:p=0.70\), \(H_a:p>0.70\); right-tailed.

"Higher than" points the alternative toward larger values, putting the rejection region in the right tail.

Problem 2 — One-Sample z-Test for a Population Mean

A filling machine is supposed to put 20.0 ounces of product into each container. A quality-control sample of \(n=49\) containers has mean fill amount \(\bar{x}=19.7\) ounces. Assume the population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma=0.8\) ounces. Test whether the machine is underfilling containers. Use \(H_0:\mu=20.0\) and \(H_a:\mu<20.0\). Test at significance level \(\alpha=0.05\).
(a) Find the test statistic \(z\).
Show solution

\(z\approx-2.63\).

\(\text{SE}=0.8/7\approx0.1143\). \(z=(19.7-20.0)/0.1143\approx-2.63\).

(b) Find the \(p\)-value (left-tailed).
Show solution

\(p\)-value \(\approx0.0043\).

Reading the z-table's left tail at \(z=-2.63\) gives \(0.0043\).

(c) State your decision at \(\alpha=0.05\): compare the \(p\)-value to \(\alpha\).
Show solution

Reject \(H_0\): \(p=0.0043<\alpha=0.05\).

Because the \(p\)-value is smaller than \(\alpha\), the observed sample mean would be very unlikely if \(H_0\) were true, so we reject \(H_0\).

(d) Write a complete conclusion sentence in context, naming the variable, the direction, and the significance level.
Show solution

The machine is underfilling.

There is sufficient evidence at \(\alpha=0.05\) that the true mean fill is less than 20.0 oz.

Problem 3 — Interpreting a p-Value

A test statistic is reported as \(z=1.84\). Use this same z-score to compare how the p-value changes depending on whether the alternative hypothesis is right-tailed or two-tailed.
(a) Find the \(p\)-value for a right-tailed test.
Show solution

\(p=0.0329\).

\(P(Z\le1.84)=0.9671\), so the right-tailed \(p\)-value is \(1-0.9671=0.0329\).

(b) Find the \(p\)-value for a two-tailed test.
Show solution

\(p=0.0658\).

A two-tailed \(p\)-value doubles the one-tailed area: \(2\times0.0329=0.0658\).

(c) State the decision for each test at \(\alpha=0.05\), and explain why the same \(z\) gives different conclusions.
Show solution
Test\(p\)-valueDecision
Right-tailed0.0329Reject \(H_0\) (\(0.0329<0.05\))
Two-tailed0.0658Fail to reject (\(0.0658>0.05\))

A two-tailed test spreads the same rejection probability across both tails, so each individual tail holds less area. The same \(z=1.84\) is extreme enough to reject in a one-directional test but not extreme enough once the test has to account for deviations in either direction.

Problem 4 — One-Sample z-Test for a Population Proportion

A company claims that 65% of its customers renew their subscription. In a random sample of \(n=200\) customers, 118 renew. Test whether the true renewal proportion is less than the company claims. Use \(H_0:p=0.65\) and \(H_a:p<0.65\). Test at significance level \(\alpha=0.05\).
(a) Find the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\).
Show solution

\(\hat{p}=0.59\).

\(118/200=0.59\).

(b) Find the test statistic \(z\).
Show solution

\(z\approx-1.78\).

\(\text{SE}=\sqrt{0.65\times0.35/200}\approx0.0337\). \(z=(0.59-0.65)/0.0337\approx-1.78\).

(c) Find the \(p\)-value.
Show solution

\(p\)-value \(\approx0.0375\).

Reading the z-table's left tail at \(z=-1.78\) gives \(0.0375\).

(d) State the decision and write a complete conclusion sentence in context.
Show solution

\(\hat{p}=0.59\). \(\text{SE}=\sqrt{0.65\times0.35/200}\approx0.0337\). \(z=(0.59-0.65)/0.0337\approx-1.78\), giving \(p\)-value \(\approx0.0375\). Since \(0.0375<0.05\):

Reject \(H_0\).

There is sufficient evidence at \(\alpha=0.05\) that the true renewal proportion is less than 65%.
