Objectives

  • Use a continuous uniform model and the fact that total area equals 1 to find probabilities.
  • Apply the Empirical Rule (68–95–99.7) to a normal distribution.
  • Convert a raw value to a \(z\)-score with \(z=(x-\mu)/\sigma\) and read probabilities from the standard normal table.
  • Explain probability as area under a density curve in the context of each problem.

Reference Formulas

\[ U(a,b):\quad \text{height}=\frac{1}{b-a},\quad P(c\le X\le d)=\frac{d-c}{b-a},\quad \mu=\frac{a+b}{2} \]

\[ P(Z>z)=1-P(Z\le z) \qquad P(a\le Z\le b)=P(Z\le b)-P(Z\le a) \]

Problem 1 — Uniform Distribution

A coffee shop fills espresso shots with amounts that vary uniformly between 1.5 and 2.5 ounces. Let \(X\sim U(1.5,2.5)\). In a uniform distribution, probability comes from area, so the rectangle must have total area 1.
(a) Find the height of the density curve and the total area.
Show solution

height \(=1\), total area \(=1\).

height \(=1/(2.5-1.5)=1\). Total area \(=\)height\(\times\)width\(=1\times1=1\), confirming this is a valid density.

(b) Sketch the density curve: describe the shape, height, and axis labels in words.
Show solution

A flat horizontal line at height 1, running from \(x=1.5\) to \(x=2.5\).

Outside that interval the height is 0. The x-axis is the shot amount in ounces; the y-axis is density. The rectangle formed has area exactly 1.

(c) Find \(P(X<2.0)\).
Show solution

\(P(X<2.0)=0.5\).

In a uniform distribution, probability equals the fraction of the interval's width covered: \((2.0-1.5)/(2.5-1.5)=0.5/1=0.5\).

(d) Find \(\mu\).
Show solution

\(\mu=2.0\) oz.

For a uniform distribution the mean is the midpoint of the interval: \((1.5+2.5)/2=2.0\).

(e) Interpret \(\mu\) in one sentence, in context.
Show solution

On average, the machine pours 2.0 ounces per shot.

\(\mu\) describes the long-run average amount dispensed if the shop pulled many, many shots — not the amount of any single shot, which still varies uniformly between 1.5 and 2.5 oz.

Problem 2 — Empirical Rule

For the standard normal random variable \(Z\sim N(0,1)\), use the Empirical Rule to estimate the requested percentages. Do not use the z-table for this problem; the goal is to reason from the 68–95–99.7 pattern.
(a) What percentage falls between \(z=-2\) and \(z=2\)?
Show solution

95%.

The Empirical Rule states that about 95% of data in a normal distribution falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean.

(b) What percentage falls above \(z=1\)?
Show solution

16%.

68% falls between \(z=-1\) and \(z=1\), leaving 32% outside. By symmetry that 32% splits evenly, so 16% lies above \(z=1\).

(c) Sketch and label the region \(P(Z>1)\): where is the shaded region, and what is its probability?
Show solution

The shaded region is the tail to the right of \(z=1\), with area \(0.16\).

Shade everything under the curve from \(z=1\) out to the right end. That tail holds about 16% of the total probability, matching part (b).

Problem 3 — z-Table Probabilities

Now use the z-table, which gives left-tail areas \(P(Z\le z)\), to find each probability. Pay attention to whether the problem asks for a left tail, a right tail, or the area between two z-scores.
(a) Find \(P(Z\le1.72)\).
Show solution

\(P(Z\le1.72)=0.9573\).

The z-table gives left-tail area directly at \(z=1.72\); no arithmetic needed.

(b) Find \(P(Z>-0.63)\).
Show solution

\(P(Z>-0.63)=0.7357\).

\(P(Z\le-0.63)=0.2643\), so \(P(Z>-0.63)=1-0.2643=0.7357\).

(c) Find \(P(-1.20\le Z\le0.50)\).
Show solution

\(P(-1.20\le Z\le0.50)=0.5764\).

\(P(Z\le0.50)=0.6915\) and \(P(Z\le-1.20)=0.1151\). Subtracting the smaller left tail from the larger one leaves exactly the area between the two z-scores: \(0.6915-0.1151=0.5764\).
