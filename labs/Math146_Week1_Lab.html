Problem 1 — Population, Sample, and Notation

A county health department wants to learn about sleep habits among adults in the county. They randomly select 120 adults, record how many hours each person slept last night, and find that this sample has an average of 6.8 hours. Use this situation to distinguish the group being studied from the smaller group actually measured.
(a) Identify the population.
Show solution

The population is all adults in the county.

The population is the entire group the health department wants to learn about, whether or not every member was actually measured.

(b) Identify the sample.
Show solution

The sample is the 120 adults who were randomly selected and measured.

The sample is the subset actually observed; it is used to estimate facts about the population.

(c) Which symbol, \(\mu\) or \(\bar{x}\), represents 6.8 hours? Explain.
Show solution

\(\bar{x}\), the sample mean.

6.8 hours was computed from the sample of 120 adults, not from every adult in the county, so it is a statistic (\(\bar{x}\)) rather than a parameter (\(\mu\)).

(d) Which Greek letter represents the unknown true average?
Show solution

\(\mu\), the population mean.

\(\mu\) is the true average sleep time across every adult in the county. It is unknown and unobservable directly; \(\bar{x}\) is our best estimate of it.

Show solution

\(\sum x_i=12\), \(\sum x_i^2=24\), frequencies 3/3/3/1, and 70% drank at least one cup.

\(\sum x_i = 0+1+2+1+3+0+2+1+0+2=12\), and squaring first gives \(\sum x_i^2=0+1+4+1+9+0+4+1+0+4=24\). Cups \(0,1,2,3\) occur \(3,3,3,1\) times out of 10, so the relative frequencies are \(0.30,0.30,0.30,0.10\), summing to 1.00. Seven of the ten students drank at least one cup (everyone except the three who drank 0), so \(7/10=70\%\).

Problem 3 — Mean and Standard Deviation

Five friends report how many text messages they sent yesterday: 12, 25, 8, 31, 14. Use these values to compute the sample mean and sample standard deviation, then think about how an extreme value would affect both statistics.
(a) Compute the sample mean \(\bar{x}\).
(b) Complete the deviation table and compute the sample standard deviation \(s\).

Show solution

\(\bar{x}=90/5=18\); deviations \(-6,7,-10,13,-4\) (sum \(0\)); squared deviations \(36,49,100,169,16\) (sum \(370\)); \(s=\sqrt{370/4}=\sqrt{92.5}\approx9.62\).

The deviations always sum to zero — that is a built-in check. Dividing the sum of squared deviations by \(n-1=4\) and taking the square root gives the sample standard deviation, \(s\approx9.62\).

(c) A sixth friend sent 500 messages. Without computing, what happens to \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\)?
Show solution

Both \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\) increase sharply.

500 is far above the other five values, so it pulls the mean upward substantially. It also sits very far from the new mean, so its squared deviation dominates the sum, making \(s\) much larger too. A single extreme value can distort both statistics — this is why the mean and standard deviation are called non-resistant measures.
