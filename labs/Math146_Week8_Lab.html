Objectives

  • Compute expected counts for a chi-squared goodness-of-fit test using \(E_i=np_i\).
  • Find the chi-squared statistic \(\chi^2=\sum(O-E)^2/E\) and its degrees of freedom.
  • Compute expected counts for a test of independence and carry out the test.
  • Use a chi-squared table to bracket a \(p\)-value and decide whether to reject \(H_0\).

Reference: Chi-Squared Tests

Test statistic: \[\chi^2=\sum\frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\]

Goodness-of-fit expected counts: \(E_i=np_i\)

Independence expected counts: \[E=\frac{(\text{row total})(\text{column total})}{\text{grand total}}\]

Degrees of freedom: goodness-of-fit \(df=k-1\); independence \(df=(r-1)(c-1)\)

For chi-squared tests, the p-value is always the area to the right of the test statistic.

Problem 1 — Goodness-of-Fit: Campus Event Preferences

A survey asks 160 students which evening event they would prefer. Test whether the four event choices are equally popular at \(\alpha=0.05\).
EventMovie nightGame nightOpen micTrivia
Observed \(O\)54503422
Write first: \(H_0\): the four choices are equally popular. \(H_a\): the four choices are not equally popular.
(a) Find the expected count for each category.
Show solution

Each expected count is 40.

Under \(H_0\), all four categories are equally likely, so each gets an equal share of the 160 respondents: \(160/4=40\).

(b) Find each category's contribution \((O-E)^2/E\).
EventMovieGameOpen micTrivia
\((O-E)^2/E\)
Show solution

Contributions: \(4.90,\,2.50,\,0.90,\,8.10\).

Each contribution is \((O-E)^2/E\): for movie night, \((54-40)^2/40=4.90\), and similarly for the rest.

(c) Find the test statistic, degrees of freedom, and decision.
Show solution

\(\chi^2=16.40\), \(df=3\); reject \(H_0\).

Summing the contributions gives \(4.90+2.50+0.90+8.10=16.40\). Since \(df=3\) and \(16.40>16.266\) (the \(\alpha=0.001\) critical value), \(p<0.001\), so we reject \(H_0\).

(d) Write the conclusion in context.
Show solution

The four campus event preferences are not equally popular.

There is sufficient evidence at \(\alpha=0.05\) to conclude that students do not prefer the four evening events equally.

Problem 2 — Goodness-of-Fit: Snack Mix Pieces

A company claims that its snack mix contains 40% pretzels, 25% cereal squares, 20% peanuts, and 15% raisins. A student counts 150 pieces.
PiecePretzelsCerealPeanutsRaisins
Observed \(O\)72342519
Claim \(p_i\)0.400.250.200.15
(a) Find the expected counts.
Show solution

Expected counts: \(60,\,37.5,\,30,\,22.5\).

Multiply the total 150 pieces by each claimed proportion: \(150\times0.40=60\), and similarly for the rest.

(b) Find the test statistic and decision.
Show solution

\(\chi^2\approx4.10\), \(df=3\); fail to reject \(H_0\).

Expected counts are 60, 37.5, 30, and 22.5; contributions are approximately 2.40, 0.33, 0.83, and 0.54, summing to \(\chi^2\approx4.10\). With \(df=3\), \(4.10<6.251\), so \(p>0.10\) and we fail to reject \(H_0\).

(c) Write the conclusion in context.
Show solution

Fail to reject \(H_0\).

There is insufficient evidence to conclude that the snack mix distribution differs from the company's claim.

Hypotheses: \(H_0\): tutoring attendance and course outcome are independent. \(H_a\): tutoring attendance and course outcome are associated.
(a) Find the expected counts.
Show solution

Expected counts are approximately: None (36.47, 13.53), Occasional (43.76, 16.24), Regular (43.76, 16.24).

Use \(E=(\text{row total})(\text{column total})/\text{grand total}\), e.g. \(50\times124/170\approx36.47\).

(b) Find the test statistic and decision.
Show solution

\(\chi^2\approx16.40\), \(df=2\); reject \(H_0\).

The full test statistic sums \((O-E)^2/E\) across all six cells, giving \(\chi^2\approx16.40\). Since \(df=2\) and \(16.40>13.816\), \(p<0.001\), so we reject \(H_0\).

(c) Write the conclusion in context, avoiding causal language.
Show solution

There is strong evidence that tutoring attendance and course outcome are associated.

This does not prove that tutoring alone caused the difference in outcomes — association is not causation.
