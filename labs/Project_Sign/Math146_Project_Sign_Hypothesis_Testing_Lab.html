Before you begin

This activity continues the Project Sign confidence-interval lab. The 108 exact object counts are treated as an empirical population, and you will use the same fixed sample of 36 values.

Interpret carefully: The calculations show how hypothesis tests work under the stated empirical-population model. They do not turn the historical table into a random sample of all aerial events.

Objectives

  • Write null and alternative hypotheses and identify the test direction.
  • Carry out a one-sample \(z\)-test for a population mean.
  • Carry out a one-sample \(z\)-test for a population proportion.
  • Interpret a \(p\)-value and compare it with \(\alpha\).
  • Connect a two-tailed hypothesis test with a confidence interval.

Reference formulas

\[ z=\frac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n} \qquad\qquad z=\frac{\hat p-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}} \]
A \(p\)-value is the probability of obtaining a result at least as extreme as the observed result, assuming \(H_0\) is true.

Part 1. Hypotheses and test direction

Choose the correct test type for each alternative hypothesis.

A. \(H_a:\mu<2\)
B. \(H_a:p>0.50\)
C. \(H_a:\mu\ne2\)

\(H_a:\mu<2\) is left-tailed, \(H_a:p>0.50\) is right-tailed, and \(H_a:\mu\ne2\) is two-tailed.

Part 2. One-sample \(z\)-test for a mean

A claim states that the mean number of objects reported is \(2\). Test whether the mean differs from \(2\).

\[ H_0:\mu=2 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu\ne2 \]

Use \(\alpha=0.05\), \(n=36\), and the known empirical-population standard deviation \(\sigma=3.028\).

\[ z=\frac{1.944-2}{3.028/\sqrt{36}}=-0.110. \] Because the test is two-tailed, \[ p=2P(Z\ge|-0.110|)=0.9123. \] Since \(p>\alpha\), fail to reject \(H_0\).

Decision at \(\alpha=0.05\):

The Week 5 confidence interval for \(\mu\) was \((0.955,2.934)\). Is the claimed value \(\mu_0=2\) inside or outside that interval?

Part 3. One-sample \(z\)-test for a proportion

Call an entry a success when it reports exactly one object. Test whether more than half of the entries in the empirical population report exactly one object.

\[ H_0:p=0.50 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:p>0.50 \]

Use \(\alpha=0.05\). In the sample, 25 of the 36 entries are successes.

For a hypothesis test about a proportion, the standard error uses the null value \(p_0\), not the sample proportion \(\hat p\).

\[ \hat p=\frac{25}{36}=0.6944, \qquad \operatorname{SE}_0=\sqrt{\frac{0.50(0.50)}{36}}=0.0833, \] \[ z=\frac{0.6944-0.50}{0.0833}=2.333, \qquad p=P(Z\ge 2.333)=0.0098. \] Since \(p<\alpha\), reject \(H_0\).

Decision at \(\alpha=0.05\):

Why does the test use \(p_0\) in the standard error?
