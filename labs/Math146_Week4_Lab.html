Objectives

  • Find probabilities for an individual observation from any normal distribution using \(z\)-scores.
  • Compute the probability that a value falls between two points.
  • Find the standard error of the mean, \(\sigma_{\bar{x}}=\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), and describe the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x}\).
  • Use the Central Limit Theorem to find probabilities about a sample mean.

Reference Formulas

For \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma)\): \(\displaystyle z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\)

For \(\bar{X}\): \(\displaystyle z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\)   and   \(\bar{X}\approx N\!\left(\mu,\,\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\)

Problem 1 — General Normal Distribution

Adult heights are modeled by \(X\sim N(67,3)\), where the mean is 67 inches and the standard deviation is 3 inches. Use this model to find where a height of 70 inches sits on the normal curve, then translate that z-score into a probability.
(a) Find the z-score for \(x=70\).
Show solution

\(z=1.00\).

\(z=(70-67)/3=1.00\). A height of 70 inches sits exactly one standard deviation above the mean.

(b) Find \(P(X\le70)\) from the z-table.
Show solution

\(P(X\le70)\approx0.8413\).

\(z=(70-67)/3=1.00\), and the z-table gives \(P(Z\le1.00)=0.8413\). About 84.1% of adults are shorter than 70 inches under this model.

(c) Sketch the normal curve and shade \(P(X\le70)\): describe what the shaded region represents and where it falls on the curve.
Show solution

Everything to the left of \(z=1.00\) is shaded, with area \(0.8413\).

The shaded region represents the proportion of adults shorter than 70 inches. It covers most of the curve since \(z=1\) is above the mean.

Problem 2 — Probability Between Two Values

A bottling machine fills bottles with amounts modeled by \(X\sim N(16.0,0.3)\) ounces. A bottle is considered acceptably filled if it contains between 15.8 and 16.4 ounces. Use z-scores and the standard normal table to estimate the proportion of bottles in that acceptable range.
(a) Find the z-scores for 15.8 and 16.4 oz.
Show solution

\(z_{15.8}=-0.67\), \(z_{16.4}=1.33\).

\((15.8-16.0)/0.3=-0.2/0.3\approx-0.67\), and \((16.4-16.0)/0.3\approx1.33\).

(b) Find \(P(15.8\le X\le16.4)\).
Show solution

\(P(15.8\le X\le16.4)\approx0.6568\).

\(P(Z\le1.33)=0.9082\) and \(P(Z\le-0.67)=0.2514\). Subtracting the lower tail from the upper tail leaves the area between the two z-scores: \(0.9082-0.2514=0.6568\).

Problem 3 — Sampling Distribution and Standard Error

A population has mean \(\mu=50\) and standard deviation \(\sigma=20\). Suppose we repeatedly take random samples of size \(n=25\) and compute the sample mean each time. This problem asks for the mean and standard error of the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\), not for one individual observation.
(a) Find the mean of the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\).
Show solution

\(\mu_{\bar{X}}=50\).

Repeated sampling does not shift the center: the mean of the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) always equals the population mean \(\mu\).

(b) Find the standard error of \(\bar{X}\).
Show solution

\(\text{SE}=4\).

\(\text{SE}=20/\sqrt{25}=20/5=4\). Larger samples produce a smaller standard error, since dividing by \(\sqrt{n}\) shrinks the spread of \(\bar{X}\).

(c) Write the sampling distribution in the form \(\bar{X}\approx N(\cdot,\cdot)\): fill in the mean and SE, then state what the CLT tells us.
Show solution

\(\bar{X}\approx N(50,\,4)\).

\(\mu_{\bar{X}}=\mu=50\), and \(\text{SE}=20/\sqrt{25}=4\). The Central Limit Theorem tells us that for a large enough sample size, the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) is approximately normal even if the original population isn't.

Problem 4 — CLT Probability for a Sample Mean

Battery lifetimes have population mean \(\mu=500\) hours and standard deviation \(\sigma=40\) hours. A quality-control team takes a random sample of \(n=64\) batteries and records the sample mean. Use the Central Limit Theorem to find the probability that this sample mean is 495 hours or less.
(a) Find the standard error.
Show solution

\(\text{SE}=5\).

\(\text{SE}=40/\sqrt{64}=40/8=5\).

(b) Find the z-score for \(\bar{x}=495\).
Show solution

\(z=-1.00\).

\((495-500)/5=-1.00\). A sample mean of 495 sits one standard error below the population mean.

(c) Find \(P(\bar{X}\le495)\).
Show solution

\(P(\bar{X}\le495)\approx0.1587\).

\(\text{SE}=40/8=5\), so \(z=(495-500)/5=-1.00\), and the z-table gives \(P(Z\le-1.00)=0.1587\). Only about 15.9% of sample means of size 64 would fall this low or lower.
